Monstera Care Guide

Monstera Deliciosa

Light: Bright indirect

Water: Every 1-2 weeks; let soil dry out halfway.

Note: Give it a sturdy moss pole. Climbing helps them produce larger leaves and fenestrations (holes).

Monstera Adansonii

Light: Bright indirect; avoid harsh sun.

Water: Keep consistently moist but not soggy.

Note: Often called the "Swiss Cheese Vine." While it trails beautifully, allowing it to climb a pole will significantly increase leaf size over time.

Monstera Standleyana

Light: Medium to bright indirect.

Water: Prefers higher humidity; water when top inch is dry.

Note: Known for its variegated "Cobra" form. It is a natural climber; without support, the leaves tend to stay small and lance-shaped.

Monstera Burle Marx Flame

Light: Bright indirect (vital for slow growers).

Water: Sensitive to root rot; use a very chunky aroid mix.

Note: A rare, slow-growing collector's plant. High humidity is a must to prevent leaf crisping.

Alocasia Care Guide

Alocasia Amazonica 'Polly'

Light: Bright filtered light.

Water: Needs constant moisture and high humidity.

Note: Known for going dormant in winter. If it loses leaves, don't toss it—keep the corm dry and warm, and it will likely regrow in spring.

Alocasia Black Velvet

Light: Bright indirect; sensitive to foliage burns.

Water: Let it dry out more than other Alocasias.

Note: A "Jewel" Alocasia. Its thick, velvety leaves hate getting wet—avoid misting directly to prevent fungal spots.

Alocasia Dragon Scale

Light: Bright indirect (mimics forest floor light).

Water: Keep slightly moist; use a well-aerating pot.

Note: The texture is purely structural. It thrives in smaller pots as it prefers being slightly root-bound compared to larger aroids.

Alocasia Micholitziana 'Frydek'

Light: Bright indirect to maintain deep green color.

Water: Very sensitive to overwatering; let top 20% dry.

Note: The velvet-textured leaves are prone to spider mites. Wipe the leaves regularly with a damp cloth as a preventative measure.

Philodendron Care Guide

Philodendron Gloriosum

Light: Bright indirect; no direct sun.

Water: Keep moist but ensure excellent drainage.

Note: This is a crawler, not a climber. It needs a long, rectangular planter rather than a pole, as the stem grows horizontally along the soil.

Philodendron Pink Princess

Light: Bright indirect is mandatory for pink variegation.

Water: Allow top half of soil to dry before watering.

Note: If the leaves start turning fully green, it needs more light. If they turn fully pink, they may die off due to lack of chlorophyll.

Philodendron Ring of Fire

Light: Bright indirect to bring out orange/red tones.

Water: Drought tolerant; water when mostly dry.

Note: A slow-growing hybrid. It creates a beautiful serrated leaf shape as it matures, especially if given consistent, stable temperatures.

Philodendron Florida Green

Light: Medium to bright indirect.

Water: Weekly, or when the top inch feels dry.

Note: A vigorous climber. The "red" stems are a signature trait. It is much hardier than its variegated 'Ghost' or 'Beauty' cousins.

Epipremnum Care Guide

Epipremnum Golden Pothos

Light: Low to bright indirect.

Water: Extremely hardy; let soil dry completely.

Note: The "gateway" plant. While famous for trailing, if you let it climb a tree or pole, the leaves can eventually grow over 30 cm wide.

Epipremnum 'N'joy'

Light: Bright indirect light is essential to keep the white variegation crisp and prevent browning.

Water: Allow the top 50-75% of soil to dry out before watering again.

Note: While it stays small and bushy easily, giving it a pole to climb will encourage the leaves to size up and the variegation to become more stable.

Epipremnum Pinnatum

Light: Bright indirect.

Water: Water when top two inches are dry.

Note: Often confused with Pothos, but these have a more structured growth habit. They are obligate climbers and need support to reach maturity.

Epipremnum Aureum

Light: Bright indirect for best color saturation.

Water: Water when leaves start to slightly droop.

Note: Very efficient at air purification. Like the Golden Pothos, it changes drastically from a small vine to a massive-leaved plant when climbing.
